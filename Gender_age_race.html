Distribution of sex, race and group of ages in New York. Most commited crimes per sex.

By Juan Santos, Adrián López 13:00 PM ET

This analysis, a panoramic view of the diverse tapestry of New York City, unveils the intricate distribution of crime across its neighborhoods, delineating patterns of arrests based on sex, race, and age groups. It serves as a silent witness, allowing us to observe how the landscape of crime evolves over time.

Within the realm of gender distribution, statistics reveal a notable discrepancy, with men accounting for the vast majority of arrests, constituting 82% of all recorded crimes. Within the male demographic, Black individuals represent the largest racial group, comprising 39% of arrests, followed by White Hispanic (22%), White (10%), Black Hispanic (7%), and Asian/Pacific Islander (4%). Among female offenders, Black individuals also constitute the largest racial group, accounting for 49% of arrests, followed by White Hispanic (24%), White (14%), Black Hispanic (7%), and Asian (6%). Across both genders, the age group of 25-44 emerges as the most prolific in terms of criminal activity, followed by the 18-24 age bracket.

The data underscores significant disparities in crime distribution based on gender and racial demographics, with men and Black individuals disproportionately represented among offenders. Furthermore, the age groups of 25-44 and 18-24 demonstrate the highest propensity for criminal activity across genders.

When examining crime categories by gender, distinct patterns emerge. Among male offenders, the most common offenses include Dangerous Drugs (32%), Assault & Related Offenses (14%), Offenses related to theft (9%), and Dangerous Weapons (7%). Conversely, female offenders are more frequently implicated in drug-related crimes (26%), assault (25%), petit larceny (14%), felony assault (11%), and trespass (5%), among others.

The data highlights variations in crime categories between genders, with men more commonly involved in drug-related offenses and assault-related offenses, while women are frequently implicated in drug-related crimes and offenses related to theft.

Future research endeavors could delve deeper into the underlying socio-economic factors driving these demographic disparities in crime, including access to resources, neighborhood characteristics, and societal influences. Longitudinal studies could track changes in crime patterns over time and assess the effectiveness of intervention programs targeting specific demographic groups. Additionally, qualitative research could provide insights into the lived experiences of individuals involved in criminal activity, informing holistic approaches to address root causes and promote community safety.
